### CHIEF COMPLAINT AND REASON FOR REFERRAL

Patient referred for end-stage hospice care services by Dr. Robert Keating, Pediatric Oncology, Massachusetts General Hospital. Thirteen-year-old male with acute lymphocytic leukemia, diagnosed May 1973 at age 5. Patient has exhausted all available treatment options including multiple chemotherapy protocols, radiation therapy, and experimental treatments. Current prognosis is terminal with estimated life expectancy of 2-4 weeks per attending physician.

Family requests home hospice care to maintain patient comfort and dignity in familiar surroundings. Mother, Mrs. Catherine Toomey, is primary caregiver with support from father, Mr. Daniel Toomey, and older sister, Mary Toomey (age 16).

### MEDICAL HISTORY

**Primary Diagnosis:** Acute lymphocytic leukemia (ALL), relapsed, refractory  
**Date of Initial Diagnosis:** May 12, 1973  
**Treatment History:**
- Initial remission achieved June 1973 with vincristine, prednisone, L-asparaginase protocol
- First relapse October 1975, treated with daunorubicin and cytarabine
- Second remission maintained until March 1978
- Second relapse March 1978, treated with experimental protocol at Dana-Farber
- Multiple subsequent relapses with decreasing response to treatment
- Cranial radiation completed August 1979
- Bone marrow transplant evaluation completed January 1981 - patient not candidate due to disease progression

**Current Medications:**
- Morphine sulfate 5mg PO q4h PRN severe pain
- Compazine 5mg PO q6h PRN nausea
- Tylenol 325mg PO q4h PRN fever/mild pain
- Multivitamin daily

**Neurological:** Alert and oriented to person, place, and time. Appropriate responses for age. Gait unsteady - requires assistance for ambulation.

### PSYCHOSOCIAL ASSESSMENT

Patient demonstrates remarkable maturity and understanding of his condition. States "I know I'm very sick and the medicine isn't helping anymore." Expresses primary concern for his parents and sister, asking "Will Mom and Dad be okay?" Shows interest in maintaining connection with school friends and completing a model airplane project he started before last hospitalization.

Family dynamics appear strong and supportive. Mother exhibits appropriate grief response with periods of denial alternating with acceptance. Father more withdrawn but engaged in care decisions. Sister Mary appears to understand gravity of situation and provides emotional support to patient.

**Environmental Assessment:** Two-story colonial home on Salem Street. Patient's bedroom relocated to first floor family room to avoid stairs. Adequate space for medical equipment. Family has arranged for hospital bed rental. Bathroom facilities accessible on same floor.

### PAIN ASSESSMENT

Patient reports pain level of 6/10 using pediatric faces scale. Describes bone pain as "deep aching that never goes away" with episodes of sharp, stabbing pain rated 8-9/10. Pain interferes with sleep and appetite. Current morphine regimen providing partial relief but patient hesitant to request medication, stating he doesn't want to "be a bother."

**Pain Management Goals:**
- Achieve consistent pain control rating 3/10 or below
- Maintain patient alertness while providing adequate analgesia
- Educate family on appropriate medication administration
- Address patient's reluctance to report pain

### SYMPTOM MANAGEMENT

**Nausea/Vomiting:** Intermittent nausea, particularly in morning hours. Limited vomiting episodes. Compazine providing adequate control currently.

**Fatigue:** Profound fatigue limiting activities of daily living. Patient requires assistance with bathing, dressing. Able to feed self but appetite significantly diminished.

**Appetite/Nutrition:** Poor appetite with intake estimated at 25% of normal requirements. Prefers soft foods and liquids. Weight loss of 17 pounds over past 6 weeks.

**Sleep Disturbances:** Frequent awakening due to pain and position changes. Requires repositioning every 2-3 hours to prevent skin breakdown.

### FAMILY EDUCATION PROVIDED

- Reviewed pain medication schedule and importance of staying ahead of pain
- Demonstrated proper positioning techniques to prevent skin breakdown
- Discussed signs and symptoms requiring immediate nurse notification
- Provided information on normal dying process and what to expect
- Reviewed emergency contact procedures for hospice team

### CARE PLAN

**Nursing Visits:** Daily visits initially, adjusting frequency based on patient/family needs and symptom progression

**Goals of Care:**
1. Maintain optimal comfort through effective pain and symptom management
2. Preserve patient dignity and quality of life
3. Support family through dying process
4. Facilitate meaningful closure and memory-making opportunities

**Immediate Interventions:**
- Increase morphine frequency to q3h around the clock with additional PRN doses
- Coordinate with physician for possible dosage adjustment
- Arrange for hospital bed delivery within 24 hours
- Schedule social worker visit to address family coping and bereavement preparation
- Coordinate with chaplain services per family request

**Monitoring Parameters:**
- Pain levels and medication effectiveness
- Respiratory status and comfort
- Skin integrity and positioning needs
- Family coping and support needs
- Signs of disease progression

### PROGNOSIS AND FAMILY DISCUSSION

Based on current clinical presentation and disease progression, patient appears to be entering final weeks of life. Family has been counseled on expected timeline and changes to anticipate. Parents verbalize understanding that cure is no longer possible and express commitment to keeping Patrick comfortable at home.

Mrs. Toomey states, "We just want him to be peaceful and know how much we love him." Family has been encouraged to continue normal activities and interactions as much as possible while respecting patient's energy limitations.
