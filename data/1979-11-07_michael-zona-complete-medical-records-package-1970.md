## CHRONOLOGICAL MEDICAL HISTORY

### **Initial Presentation - June 12, 1970**

**Chief Complaint:** Persistent fatigue, loss of appetite  
**Examining Physician:** Dr. Margaret Sullivan, M.D.  
**Age at Presentation:** 8 years, 3 months

**History of Present Illness:**  
Mother reports 3-week history of increasing fatigue in previously healthy 8-year-old male. Child complains of feeling "tired all the time" and has shown decreased interest in usual activities including bicycle riding and baseball. Appetite markedly decreased over past 2 weeks. No fever reported. Some complaints of intermittent headaches.

**Physical Examination:**  
- Vital Signs: Temperature 98.2°F, Pulse 88, Respirations 20, Blood Pressure 95/60
- General Appearance: Pale, listless child appearing younger than stated age
- HEENT: Pale conjunctiva noted, otherwise unremarkable
- Lymph Nodes: No significant lymphadenopathy
- Cardiovascular: Regular rate and rhythm, no murmurs
- Pulmonary: Clear to auscultation bilaterally
- Abdomen: Soft, non-tender, no organomegaly appreciated
- Extremities: No edema, normal pulses

**Assessment:** Possible viral syndrome vs. nutritional deficiency  
**Plan:** Complete blood count, return in 2 weeks if symptoms persist

---

### **Follow-up Visit - June 26, 1970**

**Examining Physician:** Dr. Margaret Sullivan, M.D.

**Laboratory Results from June 12, 1970:**
- Hemoglobin: 8.2 g/dL (Normal: 11.5-14.5)
- Hematocrit: 24% (Normal: 34-42%)
- White Blood Cell Count: 3,200/μL (Normal: 5,000-15,000)
- Platelet Count: 85,000/μL (Normal: 150,000-450,000)

**Clinical Notes:**  
Significant anemia and thrombocytopenia noted on laboratory studies. Patient appears more fatigued than at previous visit. Mother reports several episodes of nosebleeds over past week. Physical examination notable for increased pallor and small petechial rash on lower extremities.

**Assessment:** Severe anemia with thrombocytopenia - concerning for hematologic malignancy  
**Plan:** Immediate referral to pediatric hematology at Children's Hospital Boston

---

### **Pediatric Hematology Consultation - July 8, 1970**

**Consulting Physician:** Dr. James Morrison, M.D., Pediatric Hematology-Oncology  
**Location:** Children's Hospital Boston

**Bone Marrow Biopsy Results:**  
- Hypercellular bone marrow with 85% lymphoblasts
- Morphology consistent with acute lymphoblastic leukemia (ALL)
- Cytochemical stains: PAS positive, myeloperoxidase negative

**Diagnosis:** Acute Lymphoblastic Leukemia, B-cell precursor type

**Treatment Plan Initiated:**  
- Induction chemotherapy protocol per Children's Cancer Group Study CCG-101
- Vincristine 1.5 mg/m² IV weekly x 4 weeks
- Prednisone 40 mg/m² PO daily x 28 days
- L-asparaginase 6,000 IU/m² IM three times weekly x 9 doses

---

### **Hospitalization Records - July 15-28, 1970**

**Admission Diagnosis:** Acute lymphoblastic leukemia, induction therapy  
**Attending Physician:** Dr. James Morrison, M.D.

**Hospital Course:**  
Patient admitted for initiation of induction chemotherapy. Tolerated initial doses of vincristine and prednisone without significant adverse effects. L-asparaginase administration complicated by mild allergic reaction (urticaria) on day 5, managed with diphenhydramine premedication for subsequent doses.

**Day 14 Bone Marrow Assessment:**  
- Significant reduction in blast percentage to 15%
- Good response to induction therapy

**Discharge Medications:**  
- Prednisone taper as per protocol
- Allopurinol 100 mg PO daily
- Trimethoprim-sulfamethoxazole prophylaxis

---

### **Remission Period - August 1970 - March 1972**

**Summary of Outpatient Management:**  
Patient achieved complete remission by day 28 of induction therapy. Proceeded with consolidation and maintenance therapy per protocol. Regular monitoring with complete blood counts and bone marrow examinations every 3 months showed sustained remission.

**Notable Events:**
- September 1970: Episode of varicella, managed with acyclovir
- December 1970: Pneumocystis prophylaxis continued
- March 1971: Routine bone marrow - continued complete remission
- August 1971: Brief hospitalization for fever and neutropenia, resolved with IV antibiotics
- January 1972: Complained of increasing fatigue, CBC showed declining counts

---

### **Disease Relapse - April 1972**

**Presenting Symptoms:** Recurrent fatigue, easy bruising, fever  
**Laboratory Findings:**
- Hemoglobin: 6.8 g/dL
- White Blood Cell Count: 2,100/μL with 45% blasts
- Platelet Count: 32,000/μL

**Bone Marrow Biopsy:** 78% lymphoblasts - confirmed relapse of acute lymphoblastic leukemia

**Treatment:** Reinduction therapy with more intensive protocol including daunorubicin

---

### **Second Remission and Central Nervous System Involvement - 1972-1973**

**Summary:**  
Patient achieved second remission by June 1972. However, routine lumbar puncture in September 1972 revealed CNS involvement with leukemic cells in cerebrospinal fluid.

**CNS Treatment:**
- Intrathecal methotrexate therapy
- Cranial radiation therapy (1800 cGy)

**Response:** CNS cleared of leukemic cells by December 1972

---

### **Final Illness - January 1974 - June 1974**

**Second Relapse - January 15, 1974:**  
Patient presented with severe fatigue, weight loss, and recurrent infections. Laboratory studies confirmed second relapse with bone marrow showing 89% blasts.

**Clinical Course:**  
Despite aggressive salvage chemotherapy, patient showed poor response to treatment. Developed complications including:
- Severe mucositis requiring parenteral nutrition
- Recurrent bacterial and fungal infections
- Bleeding complications secondary to thrombocytopenia
- Progressive disease with increasing blast count

**Final Hospitalization - May 20 - June 18, 1974:**

**Admission Status:** Patient admitted with respiratory distress, fever, and altered mental status. Physical examination notable for severe pallor, hepatosplenomegaly, and lymphadenopathy.
