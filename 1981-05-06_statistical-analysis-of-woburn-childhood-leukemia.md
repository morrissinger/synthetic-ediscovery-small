## Executive Summary

This report presents a statistical analysis of childhood leukemia incidence in East Woburn, Massachusetts, for the period 1964-1981. The analysis examines whether the observed cluster of childhood leukemia cases represents a statistically significant deviation from expected rates based on Massachusetts and national surveillance data. Key findings indicate a statistically significant excess of childhood leukemia cases in census tracts served by municipal Wells G and H, with particular concentration in areas receiving water service since the wells' opening in 1964 and 1966 respectively.

## Introduction

Following the death of James Anderson on January 18, 1981, and mounting community concerns regarding childhood leukemia incidence in East Woburn, the Massachusetts Department of Public Health initiated this epidemiological investigation. The analysis focuses on acute lymphocytic leukemia (ALL) cases among children under 19 years of age residing in defined geographic areas of Woburn during the study period.

## Methodology

### Case Definition and Identification

Cases were defined as children under 19 years of age diagnosed with acute lymphocytic leukemia between January 1, 1964, and April 30, 1981, who resided in Woburn at the time of diagnosis. Case identification utilized:

- Massachusetts Cancer Registry records
- Children's Hospital Medical Center records
- Massachusetts General Hospital pediatric oncology records
- Death certificates from the Massachusetts Registry of Vital Records

### Geographic Analysis

The study area was divided into two primary zones:
- **Zone A:** Areas served by Wells G and H (East Woburn)
- **Zone B:** Areas served by alternative water sources (West Woburn)

Population data were obtained from 1970 and 1980 U.S. Census records, with interpolation for intervening years.

### Statistical Methods

Expected case numbers were calculated using:
- Massachusetts childhood leukemia incidence rates (1964-1979): 3.2 per 100,000 children per year
- National SEER program rates for acute lymphocytic leukemia: 2.8 per 100,000 children per year

Statistical significance was assessed using Poisson probability calculations and standardized incidence ratios (SIR).

### Statistical Analysis Results

**Zone A (Wells G and H area):**
- Observed cases: 8
- Expected cases (Massachusetts rates): 2.1
- Standardized Incidence Ratio: 3.81 (95% CI: 1.65-7.51)
- p-value: 0.0013 (statistically significant)

**Zone B (Alternative water sources):**
- Observed cases: 4
- Expected cases (Massachusetts rates): 3.7
- Standardized Incidence Ratio: 1.08 (95% CI: 0.29-2.77)
- p-value: 0.89 (not statistically significant)

### Temporal Distribution

The temporal distribution of cases shows clustering in two distinct periods:
- **1972-1975:** 5 cases diagnosed (4 in Zone A, 1 in Zone B)
- **1977-1980:** 4 cases diagnosed (3 in Zone A, 1 in Zone B)

This bimodal distribution corresponds temporally with documented peak contamination periods in Wells G and H.

### Age at Diagnosis

The mean age at diagnosis for Zone A cases was 4.2 years (range: 1-8 years), compared to 6.8 years for Zone B cases (range: 3-10 years). This difference suggests potential prenatal or early childhood exposure patterns consistent with residential water consumption.

## Discussion

### Statistical Significance

The analysis demonstrates a statistically significant excess of childhood leukemia cases in areas served by Wells G and H. With a p-value of 0.0013, the probability that this clustering occurred by chance alone is less than 0.2%. The standardized incidence ratio of 3.81 indicates that children in Zone A experienced nearly four times the expected rate of acute lymphocytic leukemia.

### Exposure Assessment Considerations

The geographic distribution of cases strongly correlates with the municipal water distribution system. All eight Zone A cases resided in households that received water exclusively from Wells G and H during critical exposure periods. The wells, which opened in 1964 and 1966 respectively, drew water from aquifers subsequently found to contain industrial solvents including:

- Trichloroethylene (TCE): up to 400 parts per billion
- Tetrachloroethylene (PCE): up to 43 parts per billion
- Trans-1,2-dichloroethylene: up to 75 parts per billion

### Limitations

Several limitations must be acknowledged:
1. Small sample size limits statistical power for subgroup analyses
2. Potential for incomplete case ascertainment in earlier years
3. Population mobility not fully captured in analysis
4. Lack of individual-level exposure data

### Comparison to Literature

The observed standardized incidence ratio of 3.81 is consistent with other reported environmental leukemia clusters, including:
- Rutherford, New Jersey (SIR: 4.2)
- McFarland, California (SIR: 3.6)
- Fallon, Nevada (SIR: 2.8)

## Conclusions and Recommendations

### Primary Conclusions

1. **Statistically Significant Cluster:** The East Woburn childhood leukemia cluster represents a genuine excess of cases that cannot be attributed to chance variation.

2. **Geographic Association:** The strong correlation between case distribution and municipal water service areas suggests a common environmental exposure source.

3. **Temporal Patterns:** The bimodal distribution of cases corresponds with documented periods of water system contamination.

### Recommendations

**Immediate Actions:**
1. Formal notification of the Centers for Disease Control regarding the confirmed cluster
2. Expansion of case-finding efforts to identify additional cases
3. Implementation of enhanced cancer surveillance in affected areas
